This is Jezero Crater, the landing site of the Mars 2020 Perseverance rover, currently en route to Mars.

Perseverance will land just inside the western edge of Jezero Crater, a ~40 km diameter impact crater on the wester margin of Isidis Basin.

The centerpiece of Jezero Crater is its delta, deposited as an ancient river entered a shallow lake.1 This impressive feature is the most charismatic surface expression of a long-lived regional drainage network.

In addition to hosting sedimentary features diagnostic of an ancient lake, Jezero is filled to the brim with sediment! Reasonable timescales of deposition suggest that filling the entire crater with sediment took upwards of 1–10 Myr.2 Crater-counting studies on the resistant crater floor unit suggest a minimum age of ~3.8-3.5 Ga for this sedimentary fill.2,3

The sedimentary fill presents an interesting parallel to Gale Crater, where the Curiosity rover has been operating since 2012. Gale hosts a similar (although much thicker) sedimentary sequence. In Gale, significant wind erosion has brought much of this sedimentary stack to the surface, but here it remains buried an inaccessible.

Jezero also has an outlet channel that may have been carved in stages, suggesting a long-lived lacustrine system.1,4
